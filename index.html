    Davari, D., Chen, J., & Lehto, X.
    25th Annual Graduate Student Research Conference in Hospitality and Tourism, Las Vegas     (pp.238, #264).


    Objective:
    This study aimed to detect the enjoyable activities that could lead the attendees of academic conferences to feel the festivity, not only based on what is already happening in such events but also based on what could be enjoyable.

    Work:

    • Conducted a quantitative approach
    • Identified extant and generating non-extant conference activities (filtered 35 activities in total) by examining related literature, referencing the websites of reputable conferences in different industries, etc
    • Constructed scales to measure respondents’ perceived enjoyment toward the 35 activities
    • Designed a survey for data collection
    • Utilized SAS, a statistical analysis software, to perform t-tests and exploratory factor analysis

    Highlighted Results:

    • Significant gender gaps were detected for different activities. For example, females showed higher preference for “attending meditation sessions” which indicates they prefer to have more private time, compared to their counterparts.
    • Six aspects were identified under the construct of conference leisure. (i.e., conference-extension activities, routine-travel activities, local authentic activities, serendipitous activities, self-oriented activities, conference-leisure preparations)
    • The outcomes of this research potentially contribute to the literature of conferences and help practitioners to organize more enjoyable conference experiences.

    Chen, J. & Day, J.
    25th Annual Graduate Student Research Conference in Hospitality and Tourism, Las Vegas (pp.229, #255).

    Objective:
    This study aimed at exploring whether cultural background and generations could moderate the effects of descriptive norms on nudging pro-environmental behavior.

    Work:

    • Generated hypotheses:
      • Hypothesis 1 : In nudging pro-environmental behavior, descriptive norms may have stronger effect on collectivists, while relatively weaker on individualists. Specifically, descriptive norms are supposed to have stronger effect on individuals with collectivistic cultural values for they pursue conformity and tend to conform to the majority, whereas the effect of descriptive norms is relatively weaker on individuals with individualistic cultural values.
      • Hypothesis 2 : In nudging pro-environmental behavior, descriptive norms may have stronger effect on the elder, whereas the effect of descriptive norms is relatively weaker on the younger. Specifically, in either collectivistic or individualistic cultural context, when nudging pro-environmental behavior, descriptive norms may have stronger effect on the elder due to their preference of collectivism including pursuing conformity and interdependence, whereas the effect of descriptive norms is relatively weaker on the younger due to their preference of individualism including pursuing uniqueness and independence.
    • Planed to design a scenario where a consumer faces when she just clicked on the website of a utility company and can choose between two different contract offers, one of which is buying conventional energy, the other one is buying a 50%/50% mix of renewable and conventional at a higher cost
    • Planed to design a 2 (cultural contexts: individualism, collectivism) x 3 (generations: baby boomers, generation X, generation Y) full-factorial experiment

    Implications:

    • Results of this study will help to understand how people with individualistic or collectivistic cultural values behave pro-environmentally differently by the intervention of descriptive norms.
    • Theoretically, the findings will supplement the existing body of pro-environmental behavior literature from the perspectives of cross culture and cross generations.
    • Practically, being aware of consumers’ cultural and generational background helps hospitality industry better utilize descriptive norms in nudging energy conservation, green products purchase.
